The Careers in New Zealand with the Highest Concentration of Psychopathic Traits

The Wellington Times October 23, 2025 6 min read

When we talk about psychopaths in the workplace, we're not necessarily referring to violent criminals. Workplace psychopathy involves a specific cluster of personality traits: fearlessness, charm, ruthlessness, focus, coolness under pressure, and a lack of empathy. Research by psychologists like Kevin Dutton has identified that certain professions naturally attract—or reward—individuals with these characteristics.

In New Zealand's professional landscape, these patterns hold remarkably true. Here are the careers that research suggests have the highest concentration of psychopathic personality traits.

The Top 10 Careers

1. Chief Executive Officer

At the top of the list, CEOs often exhibit traits like ruthlessness, charisma, and an ability to make difficult decisions without emotional interference. The demands of corporate leadership—making choices that may harm some for the benefit of the organisation—can favour those with reduced empathy and heightened focus on results.

2. Lawyer

The legal profession, particularly in adversarial areas like criminal defence or corporate law, rewards emotional detachment and the ability to argue any position convincingly. Successful lawyers often need to separate personal feelings from professional obligations, a trait that aligns with psychopathic characteristics.

3. Media (Television/Radio)

Broadcasting personalities often display the charm, fearlessness, and attention-seeking behaviour associated with psychopathy. The competitive, ego-driven nature of media, combined with the need to perform under pressure and seek public validation, attracts these personality types.

4. Salesperson

Sales requires charisma, persistence in the face of rejection, and the ability to persuade others—sometimes using manipulation. Top performers in sales often demonstrate the superficial charm and goal-focused ruthlessness that characterizes psychopathic traits.

5. Surgeon

Surgery demands emotional detachment, supreme confidence, and the ability to make life-or-death decisions without hesitation. While surgeons certainly care about outcomes, the best ones can operate (literally) without being paralyzed by empathy for the patient on the table.

6. Journalist

Investigative journalists, in particular, need to be fearless, willing to manipulate sources for information, and capable of exposing uncomfortable truths without emotional hesitation. The competitive nature of news gathering rewards those who can be charming one moment and ruthless the next.

7. Police Officer

Law enforcement requires coolness under pressure, the ability to make split-second decisions, and emotional resilience in the face of trauma and danger. While most officers are empathetic, the profession can attract those with psychopathic traits like fearlessness and authority-seeking behaviour.

8. Clergy

Surprisingly to many, religious leadership makes the list. Positions of spiritual authority can attract individuals who enjoy power over others, seek admiration, and display superficial charm. Not all clergy, of course, but the role's inherent authority can appeal to those with narcissistic and manipulative tendencies.

9. Chef

Professional kitchens are high-pressure environments that often reward autocratic leadership, emotional outbursts, and a singular focus on perfection. The stereotype of the temperamental, egotistical chef exists for a reason—these environments can favour psychopathic management styles.

10. Civil Servant

The rigid hierarchies and rule-focused nature of government bureaucracy can attract individuals who enjoy wielding institutional power and following procedures without emotional consideration for individual circumstances.

The Other End of the Spectrum

It's worth noting that some professions show the lowest levels of psychopathic traits. These careers typically require high empathy, patience, and genuine concern for others' wellbeing:

  • Care aide
  • Nurse
  • Therapist
  • Craftsperson/artisan
  • Beautician/stylist
  • Charity worker
  • Teacher
  • Creative artist
  • Doctor (general practice)
  • Accountant

These roles generally attract individuals who are motivated by helping others, creating beauty or value, or maintaining detailed, conscientious work—all characteristics at odds with psychopathic personality traits.

Important Context: This research reflects statistical correlations between personality traits and career choices, not absolute judgments. The vast majority of people in these "high psychopathy" professions are not clinical psychopaths—they're simply individuals whose personality traits happen to align with career demands. Success in these fields doesn't require psychopathy, though certain psychopathic characteristics can, unfortunately, sometimes provide competitive advantages.

Understanding Workplace Psychopathy in New Zealand

In New Zealand's relatively small professional community, the impact of workplace psychopathy can be particularly pronounced. Our collaborative work culture and emphasis on fairness can clash with the ruthless individualism that characterizes psychopathic traits, yet these personality types continue to rise to positions of power and influence.

Understanding these patterns isn't about demonizing entire professions or the people in them. Rather, it's about recognizing how certain career environments reward specific personality traits—and being aware of the potential downsides when those traits include reduced empathy, manipulative behaviour, and a focus on personal gain over collective wellbeing.

For employers, this research highlights the importance of balanced hiring practices and workplace cultures that don't inadvertently reward psychopathic behaviour. For employees, it offers insight into why certain workplace dynamics feel the way they do—and why some industries might be better suited to your personality and values than others.
