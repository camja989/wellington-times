Labour's Capital Gains Tax: Winners and Losers Among Wellington's Middle Class

By Jack Campbell 29 October 2025 Politics | Business

The Labour Party's surprise announcement of a targeted capital gains tax has sent ripples through Wellington households, with middle-income families grappling with what the proposed 28% levy means for their financial futures.

Unveiled abruptly on Tuesday morning after details leaked over the Labour Day weekend, the policy would impose a flat tax on profits from investment and commercial property sales from July 2027 onwards. But crucially for most Karori families, the family home, farms, KiwiSaver accounts, and shares remain exempt.

What It Means for the Median Household

For Wellington's median household—earning around $120,000 annually and typically owning their own home—the immediate impact appears limited. The family home exemption means the average homeowner faces no new tax burden when selling their primary residence.

However, the policy creates clear distinctions between different types of property ownership. A family with a second property purchased as an investment—perhaps an inherited family bach in the Wairarapa or a rental flat in Newtown—would face the 28% capital gains tax on profits when they eventually sell.

Under the proposed rules, owners would pay tax on the difference between their purchase price or the property's value on July 1, 2027 (whichever is later) and the eventual sale price, minus qualifying expenses.

The Upside: Healthcare Access

Labour has paired the tax with a tangible benefit: three free GP visits per year for every New Zealander through a new "Medicard" scheme. For families struggling with rising healthcare costs, this could represent significant savings.

Health spokesperson Ayesha Verrall noted that some New Zealanders currently pay almost $100 to see their GP. For a family of four, three free visits each could mean annual savings of up to $1,200—a meaningful sum for middle-income households facing cost-of-living pressures.

The universal nature of the policy means even property-owning families who might eventually pay the capital gains tax would benefit from the healthcare entitlement.

The Complications

Tax experts warn that several crucial details remain unresolved. Labour hasn't yet committed to whether property owners could deduct interest expenses or building depreciation costs, leaving the true impact uncertain.

For the typical Karori household that might own one investment property, the implications depend heavily on future property values and how long they hold the asset. Tax partner Robyn Walker from Deloitte noted that taxpayers would only pay when they actually sell and realize a gain, meaning they would have "the cash in hand" to pay the tax—a key distinction from wealth taxes that require annual payments.

Property Market Concerns

The policy has sparked debate about potential effects on housing affordability. Some economists suggest it might cool investor demand for residential property, potentially making home ownership more accessible for first-time buyers.

However, critics point out that similar taxes in Australia haven't prevented severe housing affordability issues. The policy also risks discouraging property investment in an already tight rental market, potentially affecting rental supply and prices—a concern for Wellington's many renters.

Political Calculations

An RNZ-Reid Research poll last month found 43% support for a capital gains tax on investment properties, with 36% opposed and 22% undecided—suggesting the policy could resonate with a significant portion of middle New Zealand.

For most median households who own only their family home, the policy represents a trade-off: potentially slower property market growth and reduced investment options against guaranteed healthcare savings and a system Labour argues will be fairer.

The policy won't take effect until mid-2027, giving voters time to assess whether the benefits outweigh the costs—and whether Labour will be in a position to implement it after the next election.

The Bottom Line for Karori Families

For the typical middle-income Wellington household:

Likely unaffected: Single-home owners with no investment properties would face no direct tax increase while gaining three free GP visits annually.

Potential losers: Families with investment properties or rental homes would face a 28% tax on future capital gains, though only when they sell. Those considering property as a retirement investment strategy may need to recalculate.

Potential winners: First-home buyers might benefit if the policy cools investor competition. All households gain healthcare access, with larger families potentially saving over $1,000 annually on GP visits.

The devil, as always, will be in the implementation details—many of which Labour has yet to reveal.

Jack Campbell is a business and economics reporter for the Wellington Daily Times, covering policy and its impact on local communities from our Karori office.
