Trump Slaps Sanctions on Russian Oil Giants in Ukraine Pressure Play

Putin shrugs off impact as oil prices jump 5%

Reuters / The Wellington Times October 24, 2025 5 min read

Wellington, 24 October 2025 - Russian President Vladimir Putin has brushed off new American sanctions targeting his country's two largest oil companies, despite the move sending global oil prices up 5% and prompting major buyers to back away from Russian crude.

US President Donald Trump announced sanctions on oil giants Rosneft and Lukoil on Wednesday, in a dramatic reversal after saying just last week he'd soon meet Putin in Budapest to negotiate an end to the Ukraine war. The two companies together produce more than 5% of the world's oil.

The sanctions have already had immediate effects, with Chinese state oil companies suspending Russian oil purchases in the short term, and Indian refiners - the biggest buyers of Russian crude shipped by sea - preparing to sharply cut imports.

Putin Defiant

Speaking in Moscow, Putin dismissed the sanctions as ineffective, saying Russia wouldn't bow to pressure.

"This is, of course, an attempt to put pressure on Russia. But no self-respecting country and no self-respecting people ever decides anything under pressure."

He warned that cutting Russian supply would push up global prices and cause discomfort for countries including the United States.

Trump responded later: "I'm glad he feels that way. That's good. I'll let you know about it in six months from now."

Summit Cancelled

The sanctions mark yet another policy flip-flop from Trump on the Ukraine conflict. He announced he'd cancelled the planned Putin summit because it "just didn't feel right."

"It didn't feel like we were going to get to the place we have to get. So I cancelled it, but we'll do it in the future," Trump told reporters at the White House.

Putin suggested the summit had merely been postponed. The two leaders last met in Alaska in August.

Ukraine Seeks More Pressure

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskiy welcomed the sanctions as "very important" but said more pressure would be needed to force Moscow to agree to a ceasefire.

The conflict continues to grind on, now in its fourth year of full-scale fighting. Russia has made clear it opposes a ceasefire, viewing it as a temporary pause that would simply give Ukraine time to rearm.

Putin also issued a stark warning about Ukraine's requests for long-range missiles from Western allies, saying Moscow's response to strikes deep into Russian territory would be "very serious, if not overwhelming."

European Action

Meanwhile, European Union leaders meeting in Brussels agreed to fund Ukraine's needs for the next two years, though they stopped short of endorsing a plan to use frozen Russian assets for a large loan to Kyiv after Belgium raised concerns.

The EU also adopted its 19th package of Russia sanctions, including a ban on Russian liquefied natural gas imports and targeting Chinese refiners and Central Asian banks.

Impact on Russia

Russian oil and gas revenue - currently down 21% year-on-year - accounts for around a quarter of Moscow's budget and remains the most important source of cash for funding the war.

However, because Russia's main revenue comes from taxing production rather than exports, the immediate financial impact of the sanctions may be somewhat softened.
