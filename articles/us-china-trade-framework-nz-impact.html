US-China Trade Framework Could Ease Pressure on NZ Export Markets

Trade war pause between superpowers may stabilise global markets as Trump and Xi prepare to meet

27 October 2025 Wellington Times Business Desk

Top American and Chinese economic officials have sketched out a potential trade deal that could bring relief to global markets, with implications for New Zealand's export-dependent economy.

The framework agreement, discussed on the sidelines of the ASEAN Summit in Kuala Lumpur, would pause steeper US tariffs and Chinese rare earth export controls - developments that could help stabilise the volatile trade environment affecting Kiwi exporters.

Key Developments

US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent confirmed talks have eliminated the immediate threat of President Donald Trump's proposed 100% tariffs on Chinese imports, originally set for 1 November. The agreement would also see China delay implementation of its rare earth minerals export licensing regime by a year.

For New Zealand businesses, any de-escalation in US-China tensions could mean:

  • More stable global supply chains for our manufacturing sector
  • Reduced pressure on commodity prices
  • Less volatility in currency markets affecting the NZ dollar

Impact on Agricultural Trade

Of particular interest to New Zealand's agricultural sector, China has signalled it will resume substantial purchases of US soybeans. While this primarily benefits American farmers, the shift could ease competition in other protein markets where NZ exporters operate.

"When China starts buying more US agricultural products, it often creates opportunities in other markets for New Zealand exporters," notes the pattern observed in previous trade negotiations.

The agreement extends a tariff truce beyond its 10 November expiration date, potentially preventing the kind of market disruption that has previously affected New Zealand's $20 billion annual trade with China.

Rare Earths and Tech Supply Chains

China's agreement to reconsider its rare earth export controls is significant for New Zealand's growing tech sector. China controls more than 90% of these materials, essential for everything from electric vehicles to semiconductors - industries New Zealand is trying to develop as part of its economic diversification strategy.

Regional Implications

President Trump, currently in Malaysia for the ASEAN summit, struck an optimistic tone: "I think we're going to have a deal with China." The two leaders are scheduled to meet on Thursday at the APEC summit in Gyeongju, South Korea - a forum where New Zealand typically has observer status and keen interest.

Chinese negotiator Li Chenggang was more cautious, saying the sides reached only a "preliminary consensus" requiring internal approvals. "The US position has been tough, whereas China has been firm in defending its own interests," Li said through an interpreter.

What's at Stake

The discussions covered multiple areas affecting global trade:

  • Trade expansion measures
  • The US fentanyl crisis
  • US port entrance fees
  • TikTok's ownership structure
  • Taiwan concerns
  • The detention of Hong Kong media figure Jimmy Lai

For New Zealand, which maintains significant trade relationships with both superpowers (China is our largest trading partner at $38 billion annually, while the US ranks third), any agreement that reduces bilateral tensions is welcome news.

Looking Ahead

The tentative framework must still be approved by Trump and Chinese President Xi Jinping when they meet on Thursday. Success could mean a more stable trading environment for small, export-dependent nations like New Zealand through 2026 and beyond.

However, with China yet to officially confirm the Xi-Trump meeting and both sides describing negotiations as "intense," New Zealand exporters would be wise to maintain contingency plans for continued trade volatility.

The stakes are particularly high given that any escalation in the US-China trade dispute typically sees both nations turning to alternative suppliers and markets - creating both opportunities and challenges for New Zealand's export economy.
