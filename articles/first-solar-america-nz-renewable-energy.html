First Solar: America's Solar Giant Shines Bright Amid New Zealand's Renewable Revolution

By Financial Desk ‚Ä¢ 1 November 2025 ‚Ä¢ 12 min read

As New Zealand accelerates toward its ambitious goal of 95 percent renewable electricity by 2030, investors and policymakers are closely watching global solar leaders for insights into the industry's trajectory. First Solar, Inc. (NASDAQ: FSLR), America's only utility-scale photovoltaic manufacturer, has emerged as a compelling case study in solar sector success, delivering impressive third-quarter results that have sent its stock soaring more than 13 percent in recent trading.

Stellar Performance in a Challenging Market

First Solar's recent third-quarter earnings announcement revealed a company firing on all cylinders. The Arizona-based manufacturer reported net sales of $1.6 billion for the quarter, representing a substantial $500 million increase from the previous quarter. This growth was driven primarily by higher module sales volumes, demonstrating robust demand for the company's advanced thin-film photovoltaic technology.

Perhaps more impressive was the company's profitability metrics. First Solar achieved earnings per share of $4.24, comfortably within its guidance range, while maintaining a healthy gross margin of 38 percent. The company's cash position strengthened considerably, with gross cash reaching $2 billion and net cash climbing to $1.5 billion by quarter's end. This financial fortress provides First Solar with substantial flexibility to navigate industry headwinds and invest in expansion.

Key Figures: First Solar now holds a contracted sales backlog of 53.7 gigawatts valued at $16.4 billion, providing exceptional revenue visibility stretching years into the future. During the third quarter alone, the company secured 2.7 gigawatts of new bookings at a base price of approximately $0.31 per watt.

Navigating Turbulence

Despite these strong results, First Solar has not been immune to industry challenges. The company recently terminated 6.6 gigawatts of bookings due to defaults by affiliates of energy giant BP, bringing total contract cancellations to 6.9 gigawatts. First Solar has initiated litigation against BP Solar Holding seeking $324 million in termination payments, underscoring the contractual complexities that can emerge in large-scale renewable energy projects.

The company also faced production disruptions in its international facilities in Malaysia and Vietnam, alongside supply chain issues at two U.S. glass suppliers that impacted output at its Alabama manufacturing facility by approximately 0.2 gigawatts. These operational challenges resulted in First Solar slightly tempering its full-year 2025 earnings guidance to a range of $14 to $15 per share, down from previous estimates but still representing robust profitability.

The American Manufacturing Advantage

What distinguishes First Solar from most competitors is its uniquely American manufacturing footprint. As the only U.S.-headquartered company among the world's largest solar manufacturers, First Solar has positioned itself as the domestic champion in an industry dominated by Asian producers. The company's advanced thin-film technology, developed in research laboratories in California and Ohio, represents what management calls "the next generation of solar power generation."

This positioning has proven increasingly valuable as governments worldwide prioritize energy security and domestic supply chains. First Solar is currently commissioning its fifth U.S. manufacturing facility, with its Louisiana plant proceeding ahead of schedule. The company has also announced plans for a new 3.7 gigawatt finishing facility, further cementing its commitment to American manufacturing.

The strategic value of U.S. production extends beyond patriotic appeal. First Solar benefits from domestic content requirements in various government incentive programs, including the valuable Section 45X tax credits for clean energy manufacturing. During the second quarter, the company monetized $312 million of these tax credits, providing a significant boost to cash flow.

For Kiwi investors and energy professionals, First Solar's trajectory offers valuable insights as New Zealand undergoes its own solar revolution. Solar photovoltaic capacity in New Zealand surged 51 percent from 2023 to 2024, with the country now boasting 787 megawatts of grid-connected solar power. Major projects like Meridian Energy's 130-megawatt RuakƒÅkƒÅ Solar Farm in Northland signal that utility-scale solar has arrived in Aotearoa.

New Zealand's renewable share of total primary energy supply reached 45.4 percent in 2024, up from 42.9 percent the previous year. With electricity demand projected to increase at least 68 percent by 2050 due to economy-wide electrification, the solar sector presents substantial investment opportunities. First Solar's experience navigating manufacturing challenges, securing long-term contracts, and managing the intermittency of solar power provides a roadmap for New Zealand's emerging solar industry.

Moreover, as New Zealand attracts foreign investment to support its renewable transition, understanding the financial performance and strategic positioning of global solar leaders like First Solar becomes increasingly relevant for local institutional investors and fund managers considering exposure to the clean energy sector.

Industry Tailwinds and Market Dynamics

The solar industry is experiencing unprecedented demand driven by several converging factors. The explosive growth of artificial intelligence and data centers has created enormous electricity requirements, with tech giants racing to secure renewable energy sources to power their operations. Electric vehicle adoption continues to accelerate, further increasing electricity demand. These trends position solar manufacturers with significant capacity and strong balance sheets, like First Solar, to capitalize on multi-decade growth opportunities.

Analyst sentiment reflects this optimism. Needham initiated coverage of First Solar with a "Buy" rating, citing the company's promising position in the U.S. utility-scale solar sector and clear pricing advantages in the domestic market. Wolfe Research raised its price target to $232 while maintaining an "Outperform" rating, while RBC Capital Markets lifted its target to $244, highlighting the company's strong performance trajectory.

Looking Ahead

First Solar's updated guidance projects full-year 2025 net sales between $4.95 billion and $5.20 billion, with the midpoint representing approximately $5.08 billion in revenue. While the company faces challenges in rebooking the capacity previously allocated to BP affiliates and may incur underutilization charges in 2026, management remains confident in securing favorable pricing for available capacity.

The company's emphasis on balancing growth, liquidity, and profitability resonates particularly well in the current economic environment, where investors increasingly prioritize sustainable business models over pure growth stories. With a debt-to-equity ratio of just 0.12 and a current ratio of 1.9, First Solar demonstrates the financial discipline that allows it to weather industry volatility while investing in long-term expansion.

CEO Mark Widmar's assertion that First Solar is "mission ready today to help power the key pillars of economic growth" reflects a company confident in its technology, manufacturing capabilities, and market position. As the global economy electrifies and nations prioritize energy security alongside decarbonization, First Solar's unique combination of American manufacturing, advanced technology, and strong financials positions it as a key beneficiary of the ongoing energy transition.

Investment Considerations

For New Zealand investors considering exposure to the global solar sector, First Solar presents an interesting proposition. The company offers pure-play exposure to utility-scale solar manufacturing without the complications of integrated energy companies or the geopolitical risks associated with China-dependent supply chains. Its strong balance sheet provides downside protection, while its massive contracted backlog offers revenue visibility rarely found in growth sectors.

However, investors should remain mindful of the risks. The BP contract terminations illustrate that even well-structured agreements can unravel, potentially impacting production utilization and financial performance. Trade policy and government incentives play outsized roles in solar economics, introducing political risk. Competition remains fierce, with ongoing price pressures and technological evolution requiring continuous investment in research and development.

Nevertheless, as New Zealand charts its course toward a renewable energy future and Kiwi investors seek opportunities in the global clean energy transition, First Solar's recent performance and strategic positioning merit close attention. The company's ability to deliver consistent profitability while scaling American manufacturing provides a compelling narrative in an industry often characterized by boom-bust cycles.

In an era where energy security, climate action, and economic growth must advance in tandem, First Solar demonstrates that commercially successful solar manufacturing is not only possible but potentially quite profitable. For investors, policymakers, and industry observers from Auckland to Wellington, that lesson may prove as valuable as the electricity these panels generate.
